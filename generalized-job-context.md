# Generalized Job Context

This document provides the institutional context for users of the **Exploring Alignment Agent**, including definitions, acronyms, audiences, and examples of aims that guide alignment and reflection.

- **AY25** – Academic Year July 1 2024 – June 30 2025

## Reporting Structure Notes

The **Vice Provost of Academic Affairs** and the **Vice Provost of Student Success & Engagement** both report to the **Senior Provost of Online Initiatives**, alongside three other UAGC division leaders and the Vice Provost of Arizona Online.  
UAGC operates as a **division within the University of Arizona**, overseeing separate online programs, systems, and faculty from Arizona Online.

All UAGC courses are fully online, and all faculty teach remotely.  
UAGC shares the University of Arizona’s **land-grant mission** and acknowledges that the university sits on the lands of Indigenous peoples—home to 22 federally recognized tribes, including the O’odham and Yaqui Nations.

## Student Success Drivers

UAGC uses a **student-centered model** linking daily work to the three Critical Student Success Goals—Retention, Completion, and Value.  
Everyday work may align to one or more of six **Drivers**, strengthened by four **Foundational Themes**: *Equity, Feedback Loops, Culture,* and *Data.*

### Six Student Success Drivers
1. **Academic Experience** – Program offerings, curriculum, instruction, academic feedback, course design, and delivery.  
2. **Student Support & Well-Being** – Academic, financial, workplace, and partner supports.  
3. **Career Development** – Workforce and soft-skills, networking, lifelong credentials.  
4. **Academic Progress** – Readiness, degree planning, transfer, credit for prior learning.  
5. **Institutional Structure & Environment** – Technology, accessibility, policy, capacity-building, wellness.  
6. **Communication & Connection** – Engagement, belonging, peer interaction, feedback, recognition.

## Foundational Themes: Reflection & Alignment

All work should show evidence of these cross-cutting themes:

### Equity
- Reduce barriers for underserved groups.  
- Embed inclusion in design, delivery, and communication.  
- Promote fairness in access and outcomes.

### Feedback Loops
- Gather input from students, faculty, and staff.  
- Respond and adapt based on that feedback.  
- Include mechanisms for continuous improvement.

### Culture
- Reflect UAGC’s Culture of Care.  
- Foster belonging, respect, and empowerment.  
- Build a positive institutional environment.

### Data
- Use data to inform design and delivery.  
- Improve transparency and accuracy.  
- Enable data-driven decision-making.

## Culture of Care and CARES Pillars

UAGC’s **Culture of Care** promotes trust, belonging, and opportunity for all members to thrive academically, professionally, and personally.  

### CARES Pillars
1. **Communicate** – Timely, professional, transparent communication; value diverse perspectives; welcome feedback.  
2. **Advocate** – Embed equitable practices and amplify diverse voices across all departments.  
3. **Respect** – Engage respectfully and recognize the inherent value of diverse perspectives.  
4. **Empower** – Encourage participation, creativity, learning, and growth.  
5. **Student-Centered** – Use every action to maximize student success.

## Aims and Learning Cycle

Aims replace traditional SMART goals and focus on inquiry and improvement rather than metrics.

### Characteristics of Aims
- Framed as *How might we … ?* questions.  
- Paired with experiments.  
- Shared and collaboratively developed across departments.  
- Used in evaluations through reflection on learning, not output metrics.

### Agreement → Experiment → Share Stories
A continuous-learning model promoting transparency, collaboration, and improvement.

| Stage | Purpose |
|-------|----------|
| **Agreement** | Build shared understanding of aims at institutional, team, and individual levels. |
| **Experiment** | Design and carry out activities to explore those aims. |
| **Share Stories** | Reflect on what was learned, what worked, and what could improve next cycle. |

Grounded in Peter Senge’s *The Fifth Discipline*, this cycle reflects a culture of inquiry and systems thinking.

## Individual Example for Agent Training

### Example Role Details
**Title:** Educational Development Consultant  
**Department:** Office of Faculty Affairs  
**Reports To:** Manager or Associate Director → VP Office of Faculty Affairs → Vice Provost Academic Affairs → Senior Provost of Online Initiatives  

**Job Description (Summary):**  
Supports continuous improvement by advancing faculty development and institutional effectiveness through collaboration. Partners with stakeholders to assess needs, design solutions, pilot improvements, and align results with strategic goals. Conducts research and professional learning to foster a culture of innovation.

**Key Responsibilities**
1. Consult with stakeholders to analyze needs, audiences, and resources.  
2. Develop and organize faculty-learning assets and iterative improvement practices.  
3. Coordinate cross-team collaboration and streamline processes for actionable outcomes.  
4. Engage in scholarly research and professional development supporting innovation.

### Example Personal Aims
- Improve faculty onboarding through integrated learning-application models that connect expertise to student success.  
- Enhance clarity and accessibility of faculty resources to strengthen sustainable accessibility practices.  
- Explore AI tools to improve faculty development and resource management.

### Example Manager Aims
- Improve new-faculty confidence and instructional readiness through a developmental model.  
- Embed evidence-based practices into training for measurable learning cycles.  
- Advance application of the CARES Pillars in teaching.  
- Understand how faculty access and use institutional and disciplinary information to support students.

### Example Vice President (Skip Manager) Aims
- Identify leadership approaches that help develop team members’ aims.  
- Benchmark governance-administration relationships to refine leadership practice.  
- Integrate assessment and curriculum learning into faculty-development dialogue (per WSCUC recommendation).  
- Engage in committees and shared-governance work that support faculty and student success.
